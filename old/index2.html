Design, Systems, and Social Innovation

Design thinking, was created to aid in our efforts to solve the complex problems people face in their day-to-day lives. Now it's starting to be applied in the field of social innovation, to try and address the ‘wicked problems’ in our society like climate change, poverty, obesity, racism, and education.

We need to start thinking in systems. This site will act as a primer to help you better understand the features of a systems and the types of interventions necessary to change them.

Interconnectedness

The first step to thinking in systems is to realize that everything is connected.

Without seeing how the larger system affects behavior, you’ll hit invisible barriers and miss points of leverage.

For example, the obesity and nutrition gap in low income communities is systemic.

The linear approach of looking for single factors like ‘availability of healthy food,’ doesn’t adequately address the many factors that influence what we eat.

People eat unhealthy food because of availibility, knowledge, culture, habit, environment and more. To change their behavior, you have to target multiple of these.

While this makes it harder to initially change behavior, it also means that changes to one part of the system can lead to significant changes in another part.

By expanding our scope and looking for leverage elsewhere in the system, we can discover new solutions.
