Social Innovation: Through The Lense of Systems Thinking

Our world is becoming increasing complex and interconnected. And so are our problems.

The linear mindset of looking for a single ‘root cause’ of a user’s problem won’t help in addressing the many ‘wicked problems’ that plague our society. To address problems like climate change, poverty, obesity, racism, and education, we need new way of thinking.

We need to start thinking in systems. This site will act as a primer to help you better understand the features of a systems and the types of interventions necessary to change them.

Interconnectedness: Systems and Courts

Interconnectedness

Interconnectedness The first step to thinking in systems is to realize that everything is connected. Without seeing how the larger system affects behavior, you’ll hit invisible barriers and miss points of leverage. For example, the obesity and nutrition gap in low income communities is systemic. The linear approach of looking for single factors like ‘availability of healthy food,’ doesn’t adequately address the many factors that influence what we eat. People eat unhealthy food because of availibility, knowledge, culture, habit, environment and more. To change their behavior, you have to target multiple of these. While this makes it harder to initially change behavior, it also means that changes to one part of the system can lead to significant changes in another part. By expanding our scope and looking for leverage elsewhere in the system, we can discover new solutions.
